Under the bed in his Castro apartment, Kevin VandenBergh hides the source of his greatest pride.

Inside a clear plastic bin are dozens of folders containing the body of his academic work.

He fans the folders across his comforter, leafing through faded typewritten pages to find one of the last papers he wrote. He begins to read aloud, marveling a bit at his own words. He was a talented writer. No denying it.

And then, suddenly, he’s angry.

“This is who you were, Kevin,” he says, his voice hard and unforgiving. “Where did you go?”

Thirty years ago, Kevin was  a psychology student at the University of Chicago. His instructors lavished him with praise. In the margins and on the backs of his papers they scrawled notes –  he was the best student they’d seen in years, so gifted that he was almost beyond critique. With his talent he could excel in graduate school, they said. He could look toward a career in academics or writing, to an exceptional future.

He started to believe them.

A year after he graduated he tested positive for the virus that causes AIDS. It was, he understood, a death sentence.

“In 1987, my life skittered off,” Kevin said. “It’s been a disaster ever since.”

Kevin is 56 now. He doesn’t work. He rarely writes. He spends hours alone in his apartment high above Market Street. Sometimes he’s there all day.

He avoids looking in that box under his bed. He tries not to think about the man he could have been, about his decision to give up.

In one of the papers he kept –  a speech he gave at his high school graduation –  he wrote that every man is responsible for his own happiness, for choosing to seek joy. Kevin wants to be that man, but he doesn’t know how.

He doesn’t know why he lived and so many others did not.

But he’s here.

Ganymede stopped taking the drugs that were holding AIDS at bay, and braced against whatever future might come.

D[g] eeply entrenched homophobia slowed federal reaction to the epidemic, allowing HIV to spread practically unfettered. In the first decade, gay rights activists and patient advocates campaigned to make the public aware that AIDS wasn’t just a gay disease and straight men and women were at risk too. By the end of the 1990s AIDS was widely understood to be a mainstream public health disaster.

But in San Francisco, it was gay men who were – and still are – most vulnerable to AIDS. And with a large population of men so at risk of infection, San Francisco in the earliest years responded faster and more aggressively to the epidemic than anywhere else in the world.

Fear and grief were channeled into actions that would have profound, lasting effects as San Francisco learned to take care of its own. A system of care now known as the San Francisco Model emerged.

T[h] he m odel  was a blend of health care and public services that was unique at the time. On the health care side, the model provided not just care but kindness to patients who often were shunned because they were gay and they had a disease that was terrifying. San Francisco General Hospital’s AIDS wards – the first of their kind in the country – were a cornerstone of the model.

Around the health care services rose a web of nonprofit agencies created by the community itself.

“AIDS was all about aiding each other and not getting aid from the people around us and the country and the government,” said Harry Breaux, 70, a member of the city’s HIV and Aging Work Group, which is responsible for assessing the needs of people growing old with AIDS.

When drugs to fight HIV and AIDS arrived in 1995, the city was among the first to get patients into care.

T[i] he drugs, called antiretrovirals, had swift impact  on individuals and on the epidemic at large. Deaths from AIDS in San Francisco dropped from nearly 1,500 in 1995 to 424 in 1997. Three years after the drugs came along, the Bay Area Reporter, a gay weekly newspaper, ran a banner headline: “No obits.” It was the first time the paper hadn’t had an AIDS death to report in more than 15 years.

Now, 20 years after the first antiretrovirals became available, doctors believe that with drug therapy people with HIV can live a near-normal lifespan. The drugs altered the course and character of an entire epidemic. Suddenly there was hope and optimism.

T he transformation was so fast, so dramatic, that doctors, public health experts and patient advocates only recently realized there would be a community of survivors, and that they would have needs that went beyond just staying alive.

T he discovery and wide distribution of drugs to fight AIDS created a subtle but significant fissure between the greater public and those who had experienced the epidemic firsthand. People started talking more about HIV than AIDS, more about living than dying.

But AIDS left lingering wounds for the survivors. The post-drug years proved, at times, only how fragile the plague had left them.

Isolation is a widespread problem with both psychological and practical complications among long-term survivors. Men without a strong network of close friends have increased risk of depression, anxiety and other mental health problems. And men without partners may have less income, making them more vulnerable to financial upsets like losing a low-rent apartment or coping with cost of living increases.

For some men who suffered extraordinary heartache during the epidemic, reaching out and forming new relationships – romantic or not – can seem impossible. Even the smallest risk of losing again can feel too dangerous.

By its very nature, isolation is difficult to quantify. Those who are isolated  often aren’t tapped into social groups or services for people with HIV.

They don’t have jobs that might offer some structure. Their parents may be dead, or they severed ties long ago. They don’t have children. Their connections are tenuous and brief – phone calls with the handful of friends who survived with them, an hour a week with a therapist, an appointment with their doctor.

Of the work being done to improve services for long-term survivors, overcoming isolation has drawn the most attention and resources.

The San Francisco AIDS Foundation  in April 2014 started the 50-Plus Network for older gay men. The members meet weekly for dinner and to talk about their pasts and their plans for the future. For some, it’s their only shared meal of the week. A handful of other informal support groups have  sprung up, but they still aren’t close to reaching everyone who needs  help.

It’s not uncommon for long-term survivors to feel older than their years. They often complain of frailty and mobility problems that typically afflict people older than those in their 50s and 60s. They have higher-than-expected rates of heart disease, diabetes and a host of other age-related disorders.

D[l] octors and scientists are beginning to understand that long-time exposure to HIV, even in those who seemed to remain otherwise healthy, may cause premature aging. Even the drugs themselves can harm the liver and kidneys, causing further aging problems.

In June, doctors at UCSF and the San Francisco Veterans Affairs Medical Center released results from a study of 155 people – mostly men in their mid-50s – who were HIV-positive. Nearly half of them were suffering cognitive impairment. More than a third had some vision loss and 14 percent were hearing impaired. One in four had suffered a fall in the previous year – a sign of frailty.

Those rates are more typical of what doctors would expect to see in men a decade or older than the ones who were studied.

Why these men are experiencing early effects of aging isn’t entirely understood, said the study’s lead author, Dr. Meredith Greene, a UCSF geriatrician who also specializes in HIV and AIDS care . But what is clear, she said, is that the type of care that long-term survivors of AIDS need is shifting.

“It’s not just about HIV anymore,” she said. “We’re dealing with multiple, chronic medical conditions.”

David Spiher spent much of his life convinced AIDS would kill him. There were times he believed he wouldn’t outlive his dog. That he’d never own a home or retire or grow old with someone he loves.

David met Ralph Thurlow 11 years ago, at a 12-step meeting for recovering alcoholics in Manhattan. Ralph was newly diagnosed with HIV and panicking. He went to the meeting, feeling scared and alone, and he stood up and said out loud that he was positive.

After the meeting, David introduced himself. And he said, “You’re going to be OK. You’re going to live.”

What David said that day was true enough – by then people with AIDS were starting to live. David himself had been living with AIDS for nearly 20 years.

They started dating about six months after they met, and they moved to the Bay Area in 2006, when Ralph got a job as a veterinary surgeon caring for animals in a research lab. They bought a small house in Hayward with a large yard of tangled gardens and dozens of animals – chickens and turkeys, rats and guinea pigs, dogs and cats. David found work as a development administrator at an Oakland private school.

They were alive and they were healthy. They made new friends, they started a new life.

One night in early 2013 they were playing cribbage, just the two of them. In cribbage, players draw cards and add up points based on the face of their cards, and Ralph pulled a five and an eight. And he got stuck.

David remembers asking him, “What’s five and eight?”

He was met with a long silence.

“Do you know what five and eight make?” David asked, gently.

Ralph said, “Give me a minute.”

“It took an inordinate time of my life, him not answering,” David said, recalling that night.

Ralph had lost his job a few months earlier. He later admitted that he’d been struggling at work, needing a calculator to add grams as he measured drugs for his animals. He’d thought it was just age, and if it was something more serious, it wasn’t anything he wanted to think too hard about.

They saw the first doctor in February 2013, kicking off months of testing and visits with specialists. It wasn’t until they happened upon a doctor who’d worked at the National Institutes of Health that they finally got an answer.

R[m] alph has HIV Associated Neurocognitive Disorder. HAND can occur when HIV breaches the nervous system. Most people suffer relatively mild cognitive symptoms that impair memory, language and decision-making skills, but they may still go about their daily lives. Ralph has a more severe form of it that causes dementia.

He will lose the ability to walk, to speak, to bathe and feed himself.  The condition is fatal.

“His prognosis is five to seven years out,” David said. “It’s a really hard prognosis.”

Some days no one but David can tell there is anything off about Ralph. Other days, Ralph clearly struggles for words, clutches at memories that have started to slip away or vanished altogether.

Ralph can still drive and shop, clean the house and take care of their animals. But in June, he lost his car keys somewhere in the house and they still haven’t found them. That month, David realized that Ralph had stopped cooking lunch for himself because he was afraid of starting a fire.

David has taken ownership of Ralph’s medical care and worked months to get him on disability. To deal with his stress, David paints. He’s produced a collection of portraits and scenes from their life, splashy, colorful oils that are both painful and beautiful.

They’re an odd couple, something they both acknowledge and enjoy. David, 57, often seems laid-back –  he wears well the rumpled look of an artist –  but he has the spit and fire of a New Yorker in him. Ralph, 56, is small and compact, his sharp eyes serious behind his glasses. He’s quiet until he’s not, and then he’ll tell his life story, slipping in wry details without cracking a smile.

Ralph would gladly take an experimental medicine – anything that might help him and add to research around his dementia.

“If there was a clinical trial I’d go on it,” he said. “I’d go on all of them.”

There are no drugs that will save Ralph – not yet. After the global AIDS community fought so hard to find antiretrovirals and other drugs to fight HIV, it feels, to David, that the survivors have been left adrift.

But David has seen miracles. His own life, that he’s here at all, is amazing. Ralph has five years, the doctors say. A lot can happen in five years.

“So on some level I don’t lose heart,” David said. “I can’t decide for myself that it’s over and done.”

In the deadliest years of the epidemic, Peter Greene used to offer his apartment in the Castro to families caring for their brothers and sons. They’d stay in a spare room, for as long as they needed, and he’d give them some small peace of mind while they worried and grieved.

His home was a safe place for these families, and for three decades it was his personal sanctuary, too. Until he lost it in 2013.

Peter lives in Palm Springs now, in a mobile home that his friends helped him buy for $60,000. It’s a “lovely” home, he says, tucked against the base of a mountain. A community of older gay men has been coalescing in Palm Springs over the past decade, many of them survivors like Peter, looking for somewhere affordable, somewhere nice to settle down.

Peter’s lucky to have a home there. He knows this. But it is not San Francisco. “I was banished from the kingdom I knew,” Peter said.

The travel agency remains open, for now. A long-time client who owns several properties in the Castro offered Peter a low-rent storefront on 19th Street. Peter oversees the business from his new home 500 miles away.

Peter is determined to come back to San Francisco. But while he’s living on a fixed income from Social Security disability , he can’t imagine how he’ll manage that. The only money he earns from Now, Voyager goes toward covering the costs of running the agency.

H[n] ousing is hardly a problem unique to AIDS survivors. But the fallout from losing an affordable apartment can be especially grim for them. When he left San Francisco, Peter had to give up doctors who were intimately familiar with his long medical history. He lost support services that don’t exist outside San Francisco.

He was removed from his closest, oldest friends – the remains of his social network .

For a week in October Peter was back in San Francisco, staying with friends who have spare bedrooms or couches. He scheduled three different medical procedures, so he could see the doctors he trusts. Two nights he went to Martuni’s, the Market Street karaoke bar, where he sang with pianists he’s known for more than a decade.

“For me, the HIV story is full of woe, but I don’t want that to be the message,” Peter said. “There was suffering. But you can see, I’m having a nice day. And this is the moment, and we’re all walking, talking, eating. We can go traveling again. Hopefully there’s more of that.”

This  year’s Pride Week was warm and cloudless in the Castro.

The days leading up to the parade are always celebratory, music spilling from bars into the sunlight, restaurants packed, young men walking with their arms slung around each other or sharing long, indiscreet kisses. There’s a carefree energy to the neighborhood during Pride, something transparent and innocent, like the Castro before AIDS. This June the excitement was more palpable than usual, amped up by the Supreme Court’s same-sex marriage ruling.

The survivors were there too, walking among the young crowds. They didn’t draw attention, though their cheeks are gaunt, their gaits stiff with chronic pain. Some bear no scars, but their eyes are hollowed and distant.

Harry Breaux was one of them. He stood at the corner of Market and Castro streets, where he volunteers as a neighborhood ambassador, handing out maps and brochures to tourists. He told them which building once held Harvey Milk’s camera shop, suggested they stop by the GLBT History Museum. He gave them directions to the Haight and the Mission.

Harry doesn’t live in the Castro. He can’t afford it. Still, his pride – in the Castro, and the history and generation it still represents to him – is earnest and intense. But it’s not home, not anymore.

“I’m  living this life, but I’m not of it,” Harry said. He glanced around at the young men to whom the Castro now belongs. “The people that have shared my life with me are dead.”

In a year or two , nearly two-thirds of people with AIDS or HIV in San Francisco will be age 50 or older. That percentage is going to keep climbing as rates of new infections drop and the population of people with AIDS stays healthy and grows older.

The San Francisco Model – once, and still, heralded as the highest standard of AIDS care – has been transformed since the darkest days. Providing services for the sick and dying is no longer a priority, simply because there aren’t enough cases. Instead, the focus has shifted to prevention.

L[o] ast year, San Francisco announced a campaign to cut new HIV infections and deaths by 90 percent by the year 2020. The end of the epidemic is in sight, public health leaders believe.

T[p] hat’s thrilling, and any work to prevent HIV and AIDS is important, said Tez Anderson, one of the earliest and most outspoken advocates for long-term survivors. He created Let’s Kick ASS (AIDS Survivor Syndrome), what may be the world’s first long-term survivor group, two years ago.

But for the very survivors around whom the San Francisco Model was built, the shift of focus can feel like abandonment. It’s older men, not young people, who make up the bulk of those living with HIV and AIDS, Tez said. And it’s clear that their needs are not a priority.

“All these programs that are focusing on the youth – we need to have parity,” he said.

Kevin VandenBergh walked into the Episcopal Church of St. John the Evangelical before the music started. It was a warm Thursday night, and the first guests were arriving for a casual dance for long-term survivors of AIDS.

Kevin had decided to go on a whim. He’d been venturing out of his apartment a little more often in recent weeks, attending a couple of social meetings for long-term survivors. Depression was still a constant distraction, suicide still an option. But he was trying.

In the church he was nervous, hands shaking, talking a bit too fast. Before the dance was a buffet dinner, and before Kevin could change his mind and run home, a man he’d met once or twice before took him by the arm and steered him to a table to eat. Within minutes, he’d convinced Kevin to march with a contingent of long-term survivors in the Pride Parade a few days later.

By the time the dance started, Kevin was feeling over-stimulated, but successful in his outing. He’d said yes – he’d ventured outside his quiet, lonely life. He danced for hours before heading home, but he wasn’t even halfway down the block before a familiar song came on and lured him back.

It was “I Will Survive” and Kevin recognized, later, how embarrassingly on-the-nose the song was for him. But it’s still a great tune, he said. He used to dance to it at the Chicago clubs in the late ’70s.

So he returned to church, and when the lights came up, Kevin was there, sweat-drenched and beaming, one of only a handful of men still dancing.

Three days later he marched in the Pride Parade, carrying a banner that said “Love Heals.”

Holding the  opposite end of the banner was John, a man Kevin had met at the dance. They’d clicked; Kevin made John laugh. They marched down Market Street together, Kevin cheering himself hoarse, waving at the crowds and pumping his fist in the air.

The parade dumped them in Civic Center Plaza, where Kevin and John were swallowed by the crowds. Kevin reached for John, so they wouldn’t lose each other.

“And all of a sudden I realized, we were holding hands,” Kevin said. It’s weeks later and his eyes are bright. “I can’t tell you the last time I held someone’s hand.”

David Spiher proposed to Ralph Thurlow in late 2013. Ralph responded with a quick and sincere no. He knew too many people with terrible marriages, he said. But the following February, Ralph proposed – on Valentine’s Day, no less.

He’d forgotten to buy a gift, David says with a grin.

Ralph says he doesn’t remember that.

The wedding was July 25. The ceremony was simple, on a sunny afternoon at a zen center in Berkeley where they both practice. They married in front of 50 friends and family – a contingent from New York, Ralph’s sister and niece, David’s coworkers. The grooms wore white shirts and pants, and prayer beads around their necks.

David gently guided Ralph through parts of the ceremony, whispering reminders to bow or turn, to grasp hands during the vows. Ralph might have just been nervous. His memory might have been fine.

In the corner was a three-tiered cake, shaped like the mountains around Yosemite Valley, a place they both love. Over one side spilled a frosted blue waterfall, and perched close to the edge was a cake topper made just for them. A friend designed a 3D-printed sculpture of David and Ralph sitting side-by-side in a rowboat, huddled close together, their hands grasping each other’s knees. The boat was suspended over the edge of the waterfall, against the current – their backs to the turbulent, unknown future.

These are not young men, said the zen master who married them, as he began the service. The crowd chuckled, and the grooms smiled too.

Turning serious, the zen master said, “These are two men who have lived a life, who understand the sadness and the suffering that’s connected with the deep well of love and passion.”

“Probably the most important thing in a marriage is to be big enough to hold everything. If we can hold it then we’re not afraid of it,” he said. “Please, in your marriage, hold your whole experience. All of it.”

Acknowledge and reflect on the largeness of life, the zen master said. Then live it, day to day, breath to breath.

“Will you?” the zen master said.

“We can,” David said, his eyes on Ralph. Ralph paused, then said it, too: “We can.”

“You can?” said the zen master.

David laughed. “We do.”

“We do,” Ralph said, only a breath behind.

In a life defined by a plague and measured in loss and pain, in fear and loneliness, sometimes it’s the smallest steps forward, the briefest moments of gratitude, that matter most. Waking up to the sunlight. Taking someone’s hand.

Saying yes.
