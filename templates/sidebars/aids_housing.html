Housing: High costs, major consequences

Housing stability is a significant problem for people with HIV/AIDS, whether they’re recently diagnosed or long-term survivors. Research has shown that people who don’t have reliable housing are less likely to stay on their drug regimen and see a doctor regularly, and have more mental health problems compared to those with stable housing.

How many long-term survivors face housing insecurity isn’t clear. The city estimates that 60 percent of gay seniors are renters, and for people with AIDS living on a fixed income, renting leaves them vulnerable to a volatile housing market. Even people who have an apartment they can afford worry about losing their homes. The AIDS Legal Referral Panel in San Francisco, which provides free or low-cost aid to people with HIV, has four attorneys assigned just to handling eviction cases, said executive director Bill Hirsh.

For those who lose a rental, finding a new affordable place can be an enormous challenge. The average rent for a one-bedroom apartment in San Francisco is about $3,500, far outside the range for someone living on Social Security disability, even with a roommate to split the cost.

There are resources to help people of all ages with HIV/AIDS find housing, but services can be complicated to navigate and there isn’t nearly enough to cover everyone in need. San Francisco closed its waiting list for affordable housing for people with HIV/AIDS more than 15 years ago when demand became too high.

A federal housing program -- called Housing Opportunities for People with AIDS -- provides up to $1,550 a month in rental subsidies. But there’s a cap on how much funding will go to a particular region, and the money sent to San Francisco isn’t expected to increase in the coming years, even as need for subsidies grows. A handful of nonprofit agencies also offer rental subsidies, but those groups don’t provide enough help to make up for the government shortfall.
